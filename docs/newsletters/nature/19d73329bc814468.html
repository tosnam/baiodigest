A ‘treasure trove’ of antiviral defences

Researchers have identified hundreds of thousands of proteins that bacteria might use to fend off viral invaders . Two research teams developed machine-learning algorithms that screen bacterial genomes to catalogue the microorganisms’ defensive arsenals. Their analyses estimated that, on average, 1.5% of a bacterium’s genes correspond to antiviral proteins — three times more than previous estimates. The teams hope that their findings could lead to the next generation of molecular tools with applications such as genetic engineering. “This is a treasure trove for any biochemist,” says microbiologist José Antonio Escudero.

Algae get their grit from giant viruses

Around 5% of the genomes of some algae ( Chlamydomonas sp. ) are made up of genes from giant viruses, which could help the organisms survive the harsh conditions of the polar oceans . Researchers found that these algae had retained more than 25,000 genes from previous viral infections, which help them to withstand freezing temperatures, fluctuating salinity and high levels of ultraviolet radiation. The team also found that the viral DNA contained many transposable elements — those that can move around the genome — which could help the algae adapt quickly to new stressors.

Symbioses can be surprisingly fragile

The relationship between the grain pest beetle ( Oryzaephilus surinamensis ) and the bacterial symbiont Shikimatogenerans silvanidophilus was forged over millions of years — but can be upended over just three generations. When researchers injected these beetles with the bacterium Sodalis praecaptivus , it quickly colonized the insects’ cells, and by the third generation had totally muscled out the original symbiont . Beetles housing the new guest had a shorter lifespan than their wild-type counterparts, which suggests the relationship isn’t as beneficial. But the findings hint that ancient symbiotic relationships are less stable than researchers had thought.

A renewed push for tuberculosis control

Almost 30 tuberculosis drugs are currently in clinical development, after decades of underinvestment. Other than during the COVID pandemic, tuberculosis is generally the leading cause of death from a single infectious disease — with lower- and middle-income communities carrying the heaviest burden. A phase III trial of the M72/AS01E vaccine is leading the pack of 16 vaccine candidates and could help make global disease control a reality.

How to save koalas from chlamydia

Koalas ( Phascolarctos cinereus ) are facing an epidemic of chlamydia, which is often fatal in these animals. Kangaroo Island — around 13 kilometres off the Australian mainland — is one of the last chlamydia-free-koala holdouts. But the genetic diversity in this population is extremely low, which could drive them to extinction. To tackle both problems, researchers are working to translocate chlamydia-free male koalas from the mainland to breed with local females . When the island’s genetic diversity has recovered, they hope to bring some back to bolster mainland populations.

Aquatic animals could be zoonotic threat

For the first time, researchers have linked a form of a human eye disease with covert mortality nodavirus (CMNV) — a pathogen that was previously known only to affect aquatic animals . CMNV causes disease in a variety of fish and crustaceans eaten by humans, but the zoonotic risk that these species pose has been largely unexplored. The new findings raise questions that might “lead to an entire new field of research”, write biologists Fabian Leendertz and Stephanie Markert. As aquatic-animal-disease outbreaks can be temperature dependent, changing climate conditions could also influence transmission of the disease, they write.
