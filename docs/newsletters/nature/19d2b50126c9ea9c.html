Spiky scales protect snakes from bacteria

Tiny ‘spikes’ on the scales of ball pythons ( Python regius ) could protect the snakes from bacterial infections. Researchers found that these spikes, each around the size of an individual cell, prevent the formation of biofilms — colonies of microbes that live co-operatively in a slimy matrix. The team theorizes that the protrusions might make it harder for bacteria to stick to the scales, or could force them into spatial arrangements that aren’t conducive to forming biofilms.

‘Zombie’ cells revived by genome transplant

Researchers have resurrected ‘dead’ bacterial cells by replacing their defunct DNA with the working genome of another species. The functionally dead Mycoplasma capricolum received engineered genomes from the closely-related Mycoplasma mycoides . If researchers can routinely make zombies from more diverse bacteria, the technique could open the door to re-engineered microbial life imbued with useful properties , such as making drugs or biofuels, through a genome transplant, or to test engineered genomes from commonly studied species, such as the laboratory staple Escherichia coli .

Infectious-disease cases flood Mozambique

Cases of infectious diseases such as malaria and cholera are surging in Mozambique after severe flooding in the south of the country damaged hundreds of healthcare facilities . The flooding has also brought some medical supply chains to a halt, leaving some people without the antiretroviral treatment they need to keep HIV in check. “It’s not just the water that took the house,” says Ana Sérgio, a resident of Chihaquelane. “It’s the fear that the children will get sick tomorrow and there will be no hospital or medicine that can reach us.”

Gut microbe boosts muscle strength

The presence of the bacterium Roseburia inulinivorans in a person’s gut microbiome appears to have a positive impact on their strength . Researchers found that people with R. inulinivorans in their guts performed better at strength-based exercises such as leg press and bench press than those that had little or none. The grip strength of mice with a depleted gut microbiome also increased after they received transplants of the microbe. The findings could lead to probiotic treatments that help people preserve muscle strength as they age, the researchers say.

A fungal transplant for trees

Last year, a crop of pine tree seedlings at a tree nursery in Louisiana were doused with a liquid containing hundreds of soil-fungus species . These fungi were isolated from the soil of thriving pine-tree forests, which the nursery staff hope will give their burgeoning trees a growth spurt — without the need for artificial fertilizers — and restore natural fungal biodiversity to their plots. The spritz is akin to a faecal microbiome transplant for people, says Colin Averill, founder of the company that produces the treatment. “We’re not trying to isolate individual pieces of the soil community; we’re taking the whole thing,” he says.

Drought drives resistance into hospitals

Dry conditions increase the concentration of antibiotic compounds in soil, which clears the competition so that resistant species can run wild. But these species don’t stay in the ground — an analysis of 116 countries reveals that hospitals in arid areas are much more likely to host drug-resistant strains . “Mitigating the drivers of widespread drought could thus emerge as a critical, if unconventional, component of preserving antibiotic efficacy for future generations,” writes microbial ecologist Timothy Ghaly. But first, we need a deeper understanding of how this soil-to-hospital resistance pathway works, he says.
