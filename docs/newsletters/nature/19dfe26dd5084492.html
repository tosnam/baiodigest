Cell structure designed with a missing piece

Researchers have engineered a bacterium that runs a key part of its cellular machinery using just 19 amino acids , as opposed to the usual 20. Previous attempts to swap out one amino acid for another have resulted in non-functional proteins. But AI helped the team to eliminate the amino acid isoleucine from the 50 proteins in a bacterium’s ribosome, while keeping it functional. The trick is a remarkable feat, experts say, and is another step in the path to building synthetic organisms.

Chatbot beats emergency room doctors

Researchers pitted OpenAI’s o1 large language model against emergency room physicians in a contest to diagnose diseases from messy, real-world patient descriptions of symptoms. They report that, at the early triage stage, the bot hit on an exact or close diagnosis 67% of the time — more than 10 percentage points higher than two doctors given the same information. The study has been praised for its realistic evaluation of diagnostic skills, and the AI’s performance is impressive, especially considering it is a 2024 model. It remains to be seen how bots perform with medical images or days’ worth of patient data.

AI energy demands debated

How much energy will AI data centres need in 2030? Two departments in the UK government disagree. The UK-based non-governmental organization Foxglove, which focuses on technology fairness, filed an environmental impact assessment request with the Department of Energy Security and Net Zero to try to unravel their estimates for AI energy use. That department’s estimate for the future energy use of the country’s entire commercial sector is 10 times lower than the amount of electricity committed to AI datacentres alone according to the Department of Science, Innovation and Technology. The reason for the disagreement in numbers isn’t clear.

How should IPCC reports use AI?

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) needs to make some big decisions about how to deal with generative AI tools, argue four researchers. IPCC reports are thousands of pages long and take years of work by hundreds of people. For the upcoming seventh assessment , due to be completed in 2029-2030, AI could help to identify the relevant literature, synthesize that information and communicate it to readers. The use of AI could help to level the playing field for non-English speakers, but it might also negatively affect public perceptions of the report. The IPCC will have to balance the risks and benefits to its scientific authority and political legitimacy, the authors write.

AIs should be given a sense of smell

AI development has focused on vision and hearing, with models able to interpret pictures or spoken words, while robotics is working on touch. What about the other senses: taste and smell? Our sense of smell helps people to evaluate how things taste, decide what’s safe to eat, when we might be in danger, whether we’re sick and even who we should pick as a romantic partner. Yet the science of olfaction is still mysterious and largely neglected in AI, notes science writer Philip Maughan.

A robot with a gentle touch

Roboticists have long struggled to give robotic hands human-like dexterity. Handling everyday objects from raspberries to keys, or completing tasks like screwing in a lightbulb, requires a robot to understand how firmly to grip an object, how to counteract the downward pull of gravity and how to recover from a fumble. Longtime Wired reporter Will Knight reports he is impressed by the startup Eka , just down the road from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, whose dextrous robotic arm learns by trying things for itself in simulations, rather than from reams of training data, and has a sense of touch. “Watching Eka’s robot in action reminds me of the first time I tried talking to ChatGPT,” says Knight.

Video: ping-pong ace is a Roboty Supreme

A table-tennis-playing robot called ‘Ace’ is one of the first machines that can best elite-level players at a physical sport . Powered by artificial intelligence, the robotic arm can calculate the position of a ball in space as it flies over the table, measure its spin and react in a tenth of the time a human player would need. To hone Ace’s skills, researchers used ‘reinforcement learning’, “which is really trial and error”, says Peter Dürr, lead engineer on the Ace project — “we put the robot in a simulation and we shoot table tennis balls at it.”
