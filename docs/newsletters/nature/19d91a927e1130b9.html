HIV PReP pill costs less than US$5 to make

The MK 8527 pill — which is currently in late-stage clinical trials — could cost less than US$5 per person per year to mass produce, according to an analysis by medical researchers. The preventative pill takes effect within 24 hours and only has to be taken once a month , offering a more accessible alternative to injections or daily pills. Advocates are calling on the drug’s developer, the US pharma company Merck, to allow a low-cost generic version of the drug to be sold in poorer countries. “To shut down HIV transmission, we need to give preventive drugs to tens of millions of people,” says pharmacologist Andrew Hill. “These drugs need to be extremely cheap to make mass prevention affordable.”

Drugs for the ‘undruggable’ KRAS protein

Fresh approaches to drug design are bringing researchers closer to effective cancer treatments that target KRAS — a protein that, when mutated, fuels some of the most lethal tumours. Mutant KRAS was once considered to be ‘undruggable’, but a new type of drug that tags the protein for destruction by the body’s ‘waste disposal’ system has shown signs of success in a clinical trial. Another four trials are also exploring a separate drug that inhibits several different mutant forms of KRAS. In combinations, such treatments could create a regimen that KRAS-mutant cancers cannot escape, says gastroenterologist Dieter Saur.

One CAR-T to cure them all

A woman with an ultra-rare combination of three autoimmune diseases has had no symptoms since receiving a single dose of engineered immune cells . Researchers used the woman’s own T cells to develop chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells that targeted a protein on her B cells — the culprits behind her trifecta of autoimmune conditions. The woman experienced no side effects from the treatment, and fourteen months on she doesn’t take any medication for her conditions, says haematologist Fabian Müller, who helped to develop the treatment.

Sibling bone marrow transplant cures HIV

A man has been functionally cured of HIV after receiving a bone marrow transplant from his sibling. The researchers found no detectable HIV in the recipient’s blood, gut tissue or bone marrow after the transplant . “I think we have shown, for the first time, that it’s a complete engraftment — both in peripheral blood, which has been shown in several other cases, in bone marrow, which has also been shown in a couple of other cases, and then also the gut mucosal tissue, which we think is key for [a] cure,” says infectious disease specialist and study co-author Marius Trøseid. A handful of people have been functionally cured of HIV using bone marrow transplants before, but the use of a sibling donor helps avoid some of the complications of a transplant.

How to achieve Alzheimer’s prevention

A group of international experts have released a framework for evaluating drugs designed to stop or slow the onset of Alzheimer’s disease. Currently, there are no approved therapies for people who are at risk or have elevated biomarkers but who are not yet symptomatic, although clinical trials are underway. The framework outlines who preventative treatments should be designed for, on the basis of factors such as biomarker levels, safety expectations and benchmarks for effectiveness. The group adds that public-private partnerships and risk-sharing will be essential to ensure treatments are kept affordable and accessible . Building the evidence around biomarkers will also be key to regulatory approval and public acceptance, they note.
