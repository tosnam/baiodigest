Microbiome can buffer peanut allergy

If a person has a peanut allergy, the make up of their gut and oral microbiomes might influence the severity of their reaction. Researchers found that certain species of Rothia bacteria in the mouth and small intestine can break down two proteins in peanuts that trigger an allergic reaction . These bacteria were more abundant in the microbiomes of people with mild peanut allergies than in those with severe ones. The team suggests that Rothia probiotics could one day be used to reduce the severity of allergic reactions to peanuts.

Virtual cell models bacterial life

Researchers have created a 3D simulation that models DNA replication, cell division and nearly every chemical reaction in a living bacterial cell . This ‘virtual cell’ is based on an organism named JCVI-Syn3a, which was created by whittling away more than 400 non-essential genes from the genome of the parasite Mycoplasma mycoides . The simulation isn’t a totally faithful recreation of the organism — the team used placeholders for some genes with unknown functions, for example. But it could help researchers understand how the mix of molecules in a cell gives rise to actual life, says computational biophysicist and study co-author Zane Thornburg.

Anti-virals put the crosshairs on HIV

A new approach to HIV treatment that triggers infected cells to self-destruct, destroying the virus in the process, has shown promise in lab studies, and some early trials. The treatment, which uses two existing anti-viral drugs, forces HIV to assemble an enzyme that the virus usually keeps broken into two halves. This enzyme triggers an internal alarm in cells where HIV persists — T cells and macrophages — which activates a cell-death pathway. The results were presented last month at the Conference on Retroviruses and Other Opportunistic Infections in Denver, Colorado.

Jump to humans helps viruses mutate

Many viruses that have caused major zoonotic outbreaks in people — including SARS-CoV-2, the virus that causes COVID-19 — only began to rapidly mutate after spilling over into people . By tracing the evolutionary history of epidemic viruses such as Ebola, influenza and the mpox virus, researchers found that the viruses gained and lost mutations at a pace similar to that of other animal viruses while in animal hosts. When these viruses made the jump into humans, this mutation rate skyrocketed, which helped the viruses spread quickly and develop into new variants.

Use AI as an assistant in microbiology

Artificial intelligence tools have already made waves in microbiology, allowing researchers to uncover potential antimicrobials and simulate outbreaks of infectious diseases. But “it is important that we envision them as assistants” that require supervision, argues an editorial in Nature Microbiology . Technical issues such as hallucinations — nonsensical and false information presented by large language models as genuine facts — are still big hurdles that must be overcome before AI models can be used in research without human oversight .

All good vaccinology comes in threes

In 2000, researchers debuted ‘reverse vaccinology’, a technique that aims to identify protective antigens in the genomic sequence of a pathogen for use in a vaccine. Then came ‘reverse vaccinology 2.0’, which uses antibodies isolated from people with infections to discover protective antigens and work out the structure of antigen-antibody complexes. Now, immunologists Emanuele Andreano and Rino Rappuoli and structural biologist Jason McLellan have described ‘reverse vaccinology 3.0’, which combines the previous two iterations with AI-enabled structural biology to accelerate the discovery of useful antigens from a years-long search to a matter of days.
