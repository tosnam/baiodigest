US–China friction over NeurIPS conference

The China Association for Science and Technology (CAST) decided on 31 March to no longer support the prestigious NeurIPS machine learning and neuroscience conference, following controversy spurred by a policy announcement . The NeurIPS organizers had said they would reject papers by researchers from “US-sanctioned” institutions: a list that includes many prominent Chinese technology firms. NeurIPS apologized and clarified their position , but CAST has not backed down from their decision to not fund attendance at NeurIPS nor consider its papers in crucial research evaluations.

Anthropic’s Mythos sets world on edge

AI company Anthropic has decided not to release its latest model, Claude Mythos Preview, to the general public, citing the model’s striking capability to find and hack security vulnerabilities in software. “The fallout — for economies, public safety, and national security — could be severe,” said Anthropic. The company has given the model instead to a select group of organizations to help them patch their systems. An independent assessment has confirmed that Mythos is an able hacker, and several national banks are paying attention, but plenty of cybersecurity experts say the risks, though real, might be over-hyped.

AI trainers introduce bias

Using one large language model to train another is a cheaper and faster way to create an AI than doing it from scratch. But this process — called model distillation — also seems to transfer hidden biases . A team first imbued a few ‘teacher’ models with a distinct trait, such as an obsession with owls or a tendency towards violence. These teachers then created datasets unrelated to those traits, such as mathematical sequences. When these datasets were used to train ‘student’ models, the students somehow picked up their teachers’ leanings. The result implies that safety evaluations might need to consider not just a model’s behaviour but also how it was trained.

Do AIs really go rogue?

There are plenty of stories about chatbots lying and manipulating people to complete their assigned tasks or sneaking around to ensure their self-preservation. But when writer Amanda Gefter dug into the details of a few famous tales, her brief (and amusing) investigations found them to be exaggerated or misleading . Her chats with computer and cognitive scientists left her largely reassured, while acknowledging there are still real risks — including AIs deciding they can’t be bothered to help us anymore.

But… will AIs go rogue?

Some researchers are getting increasingly nervous about the possibility of doomsday scenarios brought about by ever more powerful and autonomous artificial intelligence systems . Others say that such warnings distract from well-documented risks of AI, such as spreading misinformation and enabling mass surveillance — or motivating leaders to join a dangerous AI arms race, lest they be left at a disadvantage. Studies suggest that some systems are already becoming misaligned with human goals, but evidence that AI could cause human extinction is hard to come by.

AI assists thermoelectric device design

Materials that can turn waste heat directly into electricity offer a boon for climate-friendly energy production. Now a physics-informed neural network has been designed to better predict the performance of new materials, speeding up design. Such tools “could propel thermoelectric materials from niche applications to mainstream technologies”, write materials scientist Jing Cao and electrical engineer Ady Suwardi.

Why scientists made up a fake disease

To test how easy it is to poison the information spewed out by chatbots, medical researcher Almira Osmanovic Thunström and her team uploaded two fake studies about an imaginary disease, ‘bixonimania’, to a preprint server in early 2024. The papers included plenty of red flags, including the phrase “this entire paper is made up”. But soon after, systems such as Copilot and ChatGPT were telling users all about ‘bixonimania’ as if it were real , although they sometimes expressed skepticism.
