US cuts ties with global cancer agency

The United States has withdrawn from the World Health Organization this year and, as a result, will no longer support the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) — a global agency that has a 60-year history of working to prevent cancer and review the carcinogenicity of chemicals and exposures. “It’s very concerning,” says cancer epidemiologist Logan Spector. “IARC is pretty central in cancer prevention, control, etiology, and registration.” The departure of the US could mean the loss of 9% of IARC’s budget and the end of several research projects funded by the US National Institutes of Health.

Cancer cells ‘vomit up’ internal proteins

A growth-promoting cancer protein called Src has been found on the outside of a cell for the first time since its discovery in the 1980s, providing researchers with a rare potential drug target for solid tumours. Researchers traced the origin of this surface Src back to incomplete waste-removal by the cell. Instead of digesting the Src protein fully, the cell’s recycling centres called lysosomes were ‘barfing’ it out as garbage and the protein was getting stuck on the outside of the cell.

UK controversy over prostate screening

The United Kingdom’s National Screening Committee has recommended against population-wide screening for prostate cancer — a decision that was met with resistance. Celebrities and public figures have raised awareness of prostate cancer by sharing their personal stories of diagnosis, which have led to an increase in PSA testing among the public. “However, the voices of men who have suffered harm from prostate testing or subsequent treatment are seldom heard,” write cancer researcher Nikhil Mayor and urologist Hashim Ahmed. Around half of all tumours detected through prostate cancer screening would never cause harm, but their detection and treatment could cause severe side effects such as erectile dysfunction, urine leakage and rectal issues. If the government bows to public pressure and overrides the Committee’s decision, this could “cause substantial harm to thousands and place considerable strain on public health services”, they write.

GLP-1s: cancer benefits and risks

Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists — diabetes and obesity drugs like Ozempic and Wegovy — might reduce the risk of liver, throat, endometrial, ovarian and prostate cancers but increase the risk of thyroid cancer. GLP-1 drugs help people feel full after eating, and could affect cancer risk through weight loss and direct effects on target cells. In people with type 2 diabetes or obesity, “the risk to benefit ratio … is clearly favourable” because of the cardiovascular benefits and lower cancer risks associated with GLP-1 drugs, write biomedical scientists Edoardo Mannucci and Ilaria Dicembrini. However, “greater caution should be applied” for people who are modestly overweight with no comorbidities as the “specific risks of some malignancies could exceed expected health benefits”, they write.

The ‘hell’ of cancer in prison

People who are imprisoned are more likely to die from cancer than the general population in the United States. Chronic stress and unhealthy food contribute to poor cancer outcomes, as do delays in diagnosis, patchy treatment and lack of coordination with outside healthcare systems. Contributors to the Prison Journalism Project (PJP) reported symptoms being ignored or downplayed, follow up appointments being missed because of administrative failures or resourcing issues and being strip-searched and chained when seeing doctors, receiving treatments or travelling for healthcare. PJP contributors described recovering from cancer in prison as a unique kind of hell.

Big spenders join in vivo CAR T market

Healthcare companies are spending billions of dollars developing in vivo CAR T cells — the next generation of cellular therapies that are transforming cancer treatment. This technology genetically edits immune cells to give them cancer-fighting properties without ever having to remove the immune cells from the body. Pharmaceutical giants Sanofi, Bristol Myers Squibb, Gilead, AstraZeneca, Johnson & Johnson and AbbVie are all getting a piece of the action, snapping up smaller technology companies with promising in vivo CAR T cell platforms.
