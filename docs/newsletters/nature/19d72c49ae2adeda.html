Cancer saps strength from immune cells

As tumours grow, immune cells called dendritic cells are weakened by a loss of healthy mitochondria . Without these energy-producing organelles, dendritic cells don’t perform their crucial role of displaying fragments of cancer cell proteins, and the T cells are less likely to attack the cancer. Mice with skin cancer mounted a powerful anti-tumour immune response when given dendritic cells with lots of healthy mitochondria. The mice suppressed tumour growth completely when this treatment was combined with a type of immunotherapy called a checkpoint inhibitor that makes cancer cells more vulnerable to attack by T cells.

Drops made from pig semen treat eye cancer

Eye drops that contain drug-carrying molecules derived from pig semen can stop the growth of a retinal cancer in mice without affecting the animals’ vision. The drops contain tiny particles called exosomes that can penetrate the barrier around the retina so drugs can kill tumour cells without causing damage to the surrounding tissue. Researchers hope that the drops could be developed into a kinder treatment for children with retinoblastoma, which is currently treated with injections into the eye, chemotherapy or laser therapy.

Persistent viral load drives blood cancer

People who carry Epstein-Barr virus but don’t have symptoms of an infection are more likely to develop a type of blood cancer known as Hodgkin’s lymphoma . Researchers spotted the association while looking for 31 common viruses inside blood or saliva samples from more than 900,000 healthy participants using whole-genome sequencing. “Antivirals … may be worth evaluation for potential prophylactic benefit against Hodgkin’s lymphoma,” write the study’s authors.

How the DICER1 gene casts a NET for cancer St. Jude researchers have cracked a longstanding mystery around why children with mutations in the DICER1 gene are prone to a rare soft tissue cancer called rhabdomyosarcoma . The tumour cells aren’t the real offenders at all — the problem starts in the surrounding tissue. When DICER1 is faulty in the body’s normal cells, it disrupts the local environment and draws in overactive immune cells called neutrophils. In preclinical models, these immune cells responded by releasing fibrous webs made out of their own DNA called neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs). These NETs work to entrap pathogens, but the self-destructing neutrophils can also release enzymes that help tumours to grow. When researchers used an already approved drug to prevent neutrophils from rupturing, the tumour growth associated with DICER1 mutations slowed down, pointing to a potential prevention strategy. Some autoimmune diseases could be a side effect of the immune system’s fight against cancer . Researchers found that some people can slow the growth of NMDA receptor-positive tumours by producing antibodies, but that these same antibodies can also trigger autoimmune brain disease. “Our findings raise the possibility that a considerable proportion of autoimmune disease might arise in individuals whose tumours have already been eliminated by the immune system — effectively, ‘the cancer you never knew you had’,” write study co-authors cancer researcher Sam Kleeman and biochemist-oncologist Tobias Janowitz.

How the body overrules ‘selfish’ genes

Genes only exist today because they were good at replicating themselves in the past. But dial up this tendency, and ‘selfish’ genes can harm the organism as a whole. The genome balances the survival drive of individual genes with the needs of the organism by operating like a ‘parliament’ where majority rules, write evolutionary theorists Thomas Scott and Stuart West in The Paradox of the Organism , a collection of essays that explore such conflicts. Cancers are evolutionary ‘cheaters’ that escape this regulatory control , they write. “I came away feeling a pleasant discomfort at how complicated the topic is, but enlightened all the same”, writes computational biologist C. Brandon Ogbunugafor in his review of the book.
