CAR T might prevent bone-marrow cancer

Twenty people with smoldering multiple myeloma who were at high risk of developing bone-marrow cancer had no detectable abnormal white blood cells after treatment with CAR-T-cell therapy . The potential side effects from CAR T, such as neurotoxicity, need to be weighed against the benefits, because people with smoldering multiple myeloma don’t typically have symptoms. Alison Cameron, an anesthesiologist who participated in the trial and experienced serious immune-system side effects, says potentially never needing treatment again is “well worth everything I went through”.

The pump of the heart ‘beats’ cancer

In mice, the mechanical action of the heart beating stops tumours from growing — which could explain why heart cancers are so rare in all mammals . Researchers compared a non-beating heart that had been transplanted onto the necks of mice with the ‘native’ hearts in the animals. After being injected with cancer cells, the external hearts were swiftly taken over by the disease, while the beating hearts stayed much closer to cancer-free. But there is a downside: pumping also seems to stop the heart from regenerating like other tissue can.

A new playbook for childhood cancers St. Jude researchers are calling attention to a simple but overlooked fact: children’s cancers aren’t just smaller versions of adult disease. Many paediatric tumours arise from disrupted developmental processes and carry far fewer mutations, meaning the timing and cell context play a critical role in disease onset. Those differences explain why treatments built for adult cancers often fall short in young patients. The team argues for models, data and therapies designed specifically for children — and shows that understanding developmental biology is key to creating more precise, effective care for them. Reference: Nature paper (15 April) Features & opinion Peptide fad could promote cancer growth

Injectable drugs called peptides made popular by social-media influencers could increase cancer risks, lead to infection or cause heavy metal poisoning . “They’re really just unapproved illegal drugs,” says cell biologist Paul Knoepfler. Many peptides, including CJC-1295, increase levels of growth hormones and encourage tissue growth, while others, such as BPC-157, boost blood-vessel formation — creating several opportunities for tumour growth. Unapproved drugs can also be contaminated with bacteria or heavy metals, says internal medicine physician Shaila Pai-Verma who has seen people with reactions to peptides, such as rashes and poisoning, in her clinic.

The un-silencing of the X chromosome

Around half of the human population, and most women, have two X chromosomes. One of these X chromosomes is silenced early in embryonic development by coating it with an RNA molecule called Xist. The inactive X chromosome, called Xi, still plays a role in supporting growth and most embryos can’t survive without it. Deleting Xist in B cells increases the chances of female mice developing the autoimmune disease lupus erythematosus, and the loss of Xist RNA later in life can trigger a slight increase in the levels of certain proteins, causing aggressive breast tumours to develop .
