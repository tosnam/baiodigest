Listeria vaccine boosts cancer treatment

An oral vaccine of genetically-modified Listeria monocytogenes , a common food pathogen, can boost the effectiveness of immunotherapies in mice with colorectal cancer . L. monocytogenes produces a protein that binds to a common tumour receptor, which helps recruit immune cells to tumours that were otherwise flying under the radar. Researchers found that colorectal tumours shrank in mice treated with the bacteria-immunotherapy combination, but grew in mice treated with immunotherapy alone. The bacteria also didn’t spread to other organs, which lowers the risk of side effects such as sepsis.

Enzyme throws out DNA-synthesis rulebook

A newly discovered bacterial enzyme can synthesize DNA without the need for a template strand. The enzyme, Drt3b, instead uses the amino acid sequence in its own active site to mimic a reference strand of RNA . “This is a fundamentally new way that life produces DNA,” says biochemist and study co-author Alex Gao. The enzyme, which forms part of a bacterial immune-defence system, could be used as a research tool if scientists can tweak its structure to produce bespoke DNA strands, the team says.

Frogs that roam free pick up good bacteria

Amphibians that can move freely between habitats such as forests and aquatic environments are more likely to host beneficial skin bacteria than those that live in areas where physical barriers, such as roads, prevent migration. These bacteria protect the animals against the deadly fungus Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis . Researchers suggest that amphibians that move between different environments encounter a wider range of microbes and pathogens, which bolsters their skin microbiomes .

Bat buffet could spread Marburg virus

Researchers who set up camera traps in a Ugandan national park have captured a host of animals feasting on Egyptian fruit bats ( Rousettus aegyptiacus ) on film. The footage offers real-time insight into how viruses might spread : the bats are known carriers of the Marburg virus, which can cause a fatal haemorrhagic fever in people. Scientists know that viruses can be passed from bats to intermediate animals and then on to humans, but this is the first time that potential intermediates have been caught on camera, the team says.

Add fungal resistance to global strategies

Major worldwide health organizations must integrate strategies to tackle antifungal resistance in their upcoming update to the Global Action Plan on Antimicrobial Resistance , argue a group of researchers that study fungal disease. This framework should incorporate clinical, agricultural and environmental settings, and adopt a multidisciplinary One Health approach that works at local and national levels. “Acting now will save lives, protect limited antifungal treatment options, and avert repeating the costly mistakes made with antibacterial resistance,” the authors write.

Viral reservoirs drive post-infection pain

Chikungunya virus (CHIKV) can persist in immune cells in joints, which could explain why people infected with Chikungunya can experience joint pain for months or even years after they’ve otherwise recovered. Infected joint macrophages release molecules that attract killer T cells, and crosstalk between the two types of immune cells seems to drive inflammation . “The work highlights the importance of examining infection within its anatomical context,” write virologists Clive McKimmie and Marieke Pingen. RNA viruses like CHIKV lingering in specialized niches could be driving a host of chronic post-infection complications, they say.

Stopping malaria is only a matter of will

Despite the availability of effective vaccines, and a host of existing mosquito-control strategies, cases of malaria are climbing. In the run-up to World Malaria Day on 25 April, a Nature editorial calls for governments, international donors and public-health agencies to do a better job of coordinating their efforts to eliminate this disease . “Some diseases are genuinely difficult, if not impossible, to eliminate,” the editorial says. “Malaria is not one of them.”
