CAR-T cells keep HIV at bay

A single dose of immune cells engineered to root out HIV has suppressed the virus to almost undetectable levels in two people, according to research presented at the American Society of Gene & Cell Therapy conference this week. The chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells were modified to carry two surface molecules that kill HIV-infected cells , with one also stopping the immune cells from getting infected themselves. It could be decades until the treatment is widely available, but the findings are a proof of concept, say the researchers.

Autoimmune gene provides viral protection

A variant of the gene PTPN22 linked to autoimmune diseases also appears to have a protective effect against viral infections. Researchers found that in mice, the presence of this variant kick-starts the activity of natural killer cells against a type of coronavirus . Removing natural killer cells from mice without the mutation had no effect on their ability to fight off the infection, which suggests these cells are not usually involved in the antiviral response. The findings could explain why this variant is relatively common in people, despite its link to diseases such as diabetes and lupus.

‘Magic’ fungi compound curbs addiction

A single dose of psilocybin, a psychedelic compound derived from ‘magic’ mushrooms, can curb the effects of a cocaine addiction for up to six months. In a trial of 40 people who reported using cocaine at least every other day, 30% of those who received psilocybin went for 180 days without taking the drug , and those who continued used it less often than they had before. “This is significantly better than any medication ever tested to treat cocaine use disorder,” says psychiatrist Stephen Ross.

Hantavirus passengers to quarantine

Almost 150 passengers and crew members on the hantavirus-stricken cruise ship MV Hondius have disembarked to return to their home countries, where they will quarantine. How each country deals with their isolation will differ , in part because transmission of the virus between people is poorly understood. Passengers from Spain, for example, will spend one week in quarantine at a military hospital, while those returning to the United Kingdom will be monitored for 72 hours in hospital, followed by a 45-day stint of isolation at home or at a facility.

What to worry about instead of hantavirus

People in the United States are unlikely to catch the ‘Andes’ variant of hantavirus linked to a recent outbreak on a cruise ship , writes epidemiologist Gregg Gonsalves. “The real story is the collapse of pandemic preparedness in this country,” he argues, noting cuts to related research, the abandonment of the World Health Organization and a proliferation of unqualified appointees in top public-health roles . “Hantavirus is not the coming plague, but one is surely coming for us in the future. We’ve never been as exposed and vulnerable as we are now for when that moment arrives.”

Microbes eat pollution — if we let them

A growing community of synthetic biologists are using biotechnology-led solutions — mostly microorganisms containing DNA tailored for a specific function — to tackle pollution ranging from microplastics and industrial waste to soils laced with heavy metals or explosive residues. But the field is held back by concerns around releasing genetically modified organisms into the environment, and the fact that current incentives make polluting profitable while cleaning up costs money.

Cholera fights ‘one battle after another’

An arms race with bacteriophages shapes how the cholera-causing bacterium Vibrio cholerae evolves across countries. Researchers found that V. cholerae in Bangladesh lose and gain genes related to phage defence at a much faster pace than those in neighbouring India. The battle against one phage, ICP1, is particularly scrappy — the bacterium can mount a defence by making antiphage proteins, which has forced the phage to develop countermeasures. “It might well be different for other bacteria and other diseases, but when it comes to cholera, it looks like one battle after another,” write epidemiologists Indra González Ojeda and William Hanage.
