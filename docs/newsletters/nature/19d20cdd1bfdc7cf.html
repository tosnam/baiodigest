AI helps make personalized pet vaccine

Australian data analyst Paul Conyngham turned to a chatbot to help design a personalized vaccine for his dog’s cancer . His five-year-old pet Rosie was given only months to live, facing a tennis-ball-sized mast cell tumour on her leg. Conyngham paid a university genomicist to sequence Rosie’s tumour and used ChatGPT to identify relevant tumour-specific proteins. He then commissioned a university biochemist to design a personalized mRNA vaccine to promote an immune response. Rosie’s condition improved, though it’s unclear whether it was the vaccine or a more general cancer treatment that helped most. Similar treatments are in clinical trials for people with lung cancer.

Pairings added to AlphaFold database

The AlphaFold protein-structure database — a key biological-data repository founded in 2021 — is getting an upgrade. The database currently holds around 200 million predictions of individual protein structures. A research consortium is adding structural predictions for 1.7 million ‘homodimers’ : complexes made up of two copies of the same protein. In the coming weeks, they also plan to add ‘heterodimers’: complexes made of two different proteins. All of these pairings can be vital to biological function, but researchers caution that not all the predictions may be correct.

US pushes for national AI legislation

The administration of US president Donald Trump has issued a framework for a national policy on AI , which it hopes will be codified into law this year. The move follows a December executive order aiming to block state-level AI regulations. The framework calls for “protecting children”, “preventing censorship” and “enabling innovation”, amongst other things. Tech Policy Press editor Ben Lennett notes that previous efforts to preempt state lawmaking on AI have faced fierce, bipartisan opposition, but the framework’s fusing of AI regulation with online child safety may encourage broader support.

3D-printed tumours advance cancer research St. Jude researchers are using 3D bioprinting to create lab-grown models of paediatric solid tumours that mimic real tissue environments. The Patel lab uses bioprinting technology to embed rhabdomyosarcoma cells into materials that mimic real tissue, addressing limitations of traditional cell cultures and animal models. These 3D models enable controlled study of how tumour cells adapt when metastasizing from dense tissue, such as bone, to soft environments, such as the lungs. The approach offers new insights into tumour growth, treatment, resistance and relapse mechanisms — particularly for sarcomas that respond to therapy but often return weeks or months later. Features & opinion Brazil trials personalized disaster advice

Brazil is developing an AI agent that will provide climate-disaster information and advice to residents , writes Soraia Raupp Musse, coordinator of the Brazilian National Institute of Science and Technology in Simulation and Monitoring for Individual Assistance in Extreme Climate Events. Data collected by citizens, including photos and sensor readings, will be combined with disaster simulations and insights from psychology to generate personalized, understandable recommendations for action. A pilot project will explore a past disaster — the 2024 flood in Porto Alegre — as a first step to informing people about their personal flood risks.

AI medical hypotheses hit clinical trials

A compound called rentosertib, which has had promising results in phase II clinical trials against the lung disease idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, could earn a place in history as the first true medicine of the AI age. Both the drug target and the small-molecule compound were identified by generative AI. Researchers are now using a variety of tools, including Google’s AI co-scientist, to generate ideas for research and suggest drug candidates for various conditions. While many of these ideas remain unproven, regulators are scrambling to account for AI in the drug-discovery process.

Formal maths challenge gears up

The ‘ First Proof challenge’ has launched a second round to test whether AIs can write mathematical proofs. In the first round , a team of 11 mathematicians picked 10 ‘lemmas’ — intermediate propositions in a larger theorem — that they had written but not yet released, and gave competitors one week to prove them using AI. Groups from both OpenAI and Google participated this February. The results were subject to interpretation, but as many as eight of the 10 problems were solved at least partially by AI. The second, more formal round of competition will be graded in June.
