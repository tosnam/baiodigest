Skincare chemical thwarts resistant E. coli

The plant chemical madecassic acid — a common ingredient in skincare products — can block the growth of drug-resistant strains of Escherichia coli . Researchers found that the molecule blocks the activity of cytochrome bd , a protein complex involved in the bacteria’s energy-making process. The structure of madecassic acid can also be easily modified, which could help scientists optimize its antimicrobial properties, the team says.

Lenacapavir to get further-reaching rollout

The US Department of State and The Global Fund have announced joint plans to scale up their rollout of lenacapavir — a treatment that can prevent HIV infection for 6 months with one injection — over the next 3 years. The organizations had previously pledged to make the treatment available to 2 million people across nine African countries. The new commitment pushes the total number of people they aim to reach to 3 million, and expands their support to 15 additional countries , including some in Europe and southeast Asia. Meanwhile, the humanitarian organization Médecins Sans Frontières is not happy that the US drug company Gilead Sciences, which makes lenacapavir, has turned down its requests to buy more doses on top of what it gets through The Global Fund.

Viruses allegedly stolen from lab in Brazil

A researcher was arrested last month for allegedly taking samples of viruses — reportedly including chikungunya and dengue — from a high-security biosafety laboratory at a leading Brazilian university. The samples were recovered, and were not dangerous, but the event has the Brazilian virology community “perplexed”, says virologist Paulo Sanches. “No sample can be removed from a lab with this biosafety level without authorization.” The news comes as Brazil is building support for the construction of its first BSL-4 lab — the highest level of biosafety facility — only a few kilometres away from the campus where the samples went missing.

Pesticides might harm our gut microbes

Evidence is growing that pesticides might disrupt the gut microbiome — and could be linked to the global rise of type 2 diabetes . A study in southern India found a surprisingly high prevalence of the disease (16%) in people who lived in the countryside, despite a lack of risk factors such as obesity. And the same team found that, in mice, a popular agricultural insecticide called chlorpyrifos seemed to repress populations of helpful bacteria in favour of potentially harmful ones, and the mice developed diabetes. “It’s not one single mechanism. It’s a network of biological effects that can ultimately disrupt communication along the gut–brain axis,” says neuroscientist John Cryan — but it’s a huge challenge to prove that pesticides are definitely the cause of harm in humans.

Home-grown fungal conservation in Africa

As the first mycologist working in Madagascar who is from the island, Anna Ralaiveloarisoa works to better understand the abundance and variety of fungal species in her home country. She is one of many African scientists stepping up to champion fungal conservation across the continent . “Africa is still finding its way in fungal conservation”, says mycologist Joyce Jefwa, who works in Kenya. But milestones such as the International Congress on Fungal Conservation, which was hosted in Benin last year, indicate that the global scientific community is beginning to recognize Africa’s role in the conservation movement.

Prevent outbreaks, don’t just contain them

Many global-health strategies still focus on responding to outbreaks stemming from animal-to-human transmission, rather than preventing this spillover in the first place, argue a group of health researchers. The group have co-founded PREZODE, an initiative focussed on reducing the root cause of zoonotic spillover . Bringing together international scientists, policymakers and community leaders, PREZODE has drawn up a roadmap that lays out particular areas of focus over the next decade to reduce the threat of zoonoses.

There’s nothing basic about ‘basic research’

Microbial-derived technologies such as CRISPR–Cas9 gene-editing tools and vaccines have transformed entire fields of research. But all of these findings are underpinned by years of basic, discovery-driven science, which can have a less obvious impact on human lives, argues a Nature Microbiology editorial. Environmental microbiology in particular relies on research , which is still only scratching the surface of characterizing the microbes in ecosystems from deserts to geysers. These microbes could have properties that are extremely useful to humans, but “this potential cannot be harnessed if basic biology remains uncharacterized”, says the editorial.
