T-cell engager shrinks prostate cancer

A type of immunotherapy called a T-cell engager, which uses an engineered antibody to connect T cells to tumour cells, has shrunk hard-to-treat prostate tumours in an early-stage trial. Researchers tested the therapy, called VIR-5500, on 58 people with advanced, treatment-resistant prostate cancer and found that tumours shrank in five out of 11 people who were given the highest dose. One 63-year-old study participant had 14 liver metastases disappear after six rounds of treatment. The study has not yet been peer reviewed and was presented at the American Society of Clinical Oncology genitourinary cancers symposium. “We do need more data but the results are stunning,” says cancer researcher and co-author Johann de Bono.

Breast cancer pill fails at the final hurdle

The oral selective estrogen receptor degrader (SERD) drug giredestrant — which was poised to be drug company Roche’s biggest seller — has fallen short in a phase III clinical trial as a first-line treatment. The drug was tested in combination with Pfizer’s targeted therapy Ibrance in patients with metastatic estrogen receptor-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer. It showed no statistically significant improvement in progression-free survival over the control group, who received Ibrance and hormone therapy. Sales of the drug were forecast to reach US$7.4 billion by 2034. The failure “pulls the rug from [Roche’s] multi-billion dollar giredestrant case”, say analysts.

Top of the pops 2025 The success of GLP-1 receptor agonist tirzepatide products for type 2 diabetes and weight management shot drug company Eli Lilly to pole position in 2025 in terms of sales of prescription drugs (US$60.8 billion). The company was not featured in the top ten in 2024, but its annual sales grew 49% last year. ( Nature Reviews Drug Discovery | 5 min read ) Features and opinion We’ve forgotten the value of antibiotics

Antibiotics are the cornerstone of modern medicine, but poor financial returns means they don't get as much investment as they should, argue synthetic biologist Cesar de la Fuente-Nunez and microbiologist Henry Skinner. Only 11 antibiotics that are currently in the clinical pipeline treat the most critical global bacterial threats . AI has flung open the door to innovation in the area — to find ways to target antibiotic resistance — but innovation alone does not a drug make. Models such as subscription-style reimbursement — where governments pay for access rather than volume — or market-entry rewards offer promising solutions, write the authors. But global adoption is needed to avoid a continued decline of the pipeline, leaving promising targets in the “dustbin of academia” and causing millions of preventable deaths.

The argument for digital endpoints

A fear of novelty is preventing drug companies from adopting digital clinical trial endpoints, which could be a powerful lever to improve efficiency and effectiveness, argue members of a global non-profit digital medicine society. Endpoints in clinical trials have traditionally relied on infrequent, clinic-based assessments that are subjective and can be burdensome for patients. Conversely, digital endpoints are derived from high-resolution, objective data captured by sensor-based digital health technologies during patients’ everyday lives. They could reduce trial size, accelerate timelines and generate valuable insights for researchers, regulators, clinicians, patients and funders, write the authors.

How and why to filter men out of the data

Across many conditions, women’s health is uncharted scientific territory. Until the 1990s, women were rarely included in clinical studies and female animals were excluded from studies using model organisms because of a prevailing belief — now discredited — that hormonal fluctuations made their biology too complicated. Disaggregating data by sex is a powerful way to help fill in the blanks — but researchers say it’s not used enough.

Buy in to rare genetic disease treatment

Extremely rare genetic mutations have such a low prevalence that most current drug discovery technologies, paths to approval and approaches to pricing do not deliver sufficient return on investment to convince drug companies to buy in. Commercialization of these therapies needs new regulation and novel payment and financing models to conventional drugs, argue pharmacologist Stanley Crooke and economist Andrew Lo. This could include subscription models or payment based on the outcome of therapies .
