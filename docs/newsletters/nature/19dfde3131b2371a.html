FDA to speed up approvals with live data

The US Food and Drug administration (FDA) is moving to make clinical trials more efficient, by reviewing data in real time and possibly working with companies which use AI for monitoring and recruitment. Two early-stage clinical trials by drug companies AstraZeneca and Amgen for lymphoma and small cell lung carcinoma, respectively, will use the real-time data platform built by Paradigm to allow FDA reviewers live access to their clinical data . The broader goal is to allow for more continuous trials without pauses between phases and to incentivise running trials in the United States rather than China or Australia.

Light-sensitive cancer drugs win $2 million

Researchers have won a US$2-million prize for demonstrating that future quantum computers could simulate the chemical reactions that drive light-sensitive cancer drugs in ways that would be impossible using classical computing. These drugs can be activated using light once they reach a tumour , minimizing toxicity to the rest of the body. The US$2-million Quantum for Bio prize was awarded to researchers from quantum software firm Algorithmiq, technology company IBM and the Cleveland Clinic hospital.

The lay of the land for cancer vaccination

The progress of cancer vaccines has been limited by immune tolerance, variability in how well antibodies can attach to targets and modest clinical efficacy. But recent advances in mRNA vaccines, better ability to predict cancer-specific targets and combination with immunotherapies are helping to make vaccines more precise and effective. As of May 2025, there are 513 cancer vaccines in development, with most (47%) in early-stage development . Continued advancement will require more accurate ways to predict new proteins on cancer cells that can be targeted by antibodies, improved delivery systems and integration with other forms of therapy, suggest a group of health and drug researchers.

The real potential of stem cells

Partial reprogramming — the ability to switch genes on and off to make cells temporarily revert to a stem cell-like state — is being tested for the first time in humans. The trial of 18 people will see participants with degenerative eye conditions injected with a non-infectious virus containing factors that induce a stem cell-like state in cells in the eye to aid rejuvenation. These factors will be activated for 56 days using an oral drug and then switched off. The trial is one in a long line aimed at reversing the impacts of aging, many of which have so far failed. “If just one company becomes successful, given that slowing down ageing would impact so much in medicine and society, that would be transformative,” said molecular biologist João Pedro de Magalhães in 2019, when the idea of partial reprogramming was in its earlier days.

Brain stim could expand psychiatry's toolkit

Since the 1980s, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) have been psychiatry's go-to depression treatment. Now, a small electric device could be beginning to change that. Transcranial direct-current stimulation (tDCS) uses a weak electric current to shock the brain, mildly reducing symptoms of depression. A tDCS device, which has been approved in England since 2019, was approved in the US in December as part of a movement to expand psychiatry's toolkit beyond SSRIs. The device can be used in combination with drugs , but may also push psychiatrists to try treatments beyond SSRIs, which are currently taken by around one in six people in the United States.
