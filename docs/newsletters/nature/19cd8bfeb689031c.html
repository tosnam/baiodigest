AI on the battlefield

The escalating conflict between the United States, Israel and Iran has thrown a spotlight on the use of artificial intelligence in warfare . The US military uses AI from tech company Anthropic. But constraints on future potential uses for AI are at the heart of a disagreement between the US Department of Defense and Anthropic: Anthropic, along with many other companies and researchers, do not want AI used in mass domestic surveillance or in fully autonomous weapons without proper oversight. Meanwhile, international talks are exploring the possible regulation of AI in warfare: experts met in Geneva last week, for example, to discuss lethal autonomous weapons. But these efforts are hamstrung by lack of a lack of engagement from the main players, including the US, Israel and China.

Anthropic declared ‘supply chain risk'

The US government has officially declared Anthropic a “supply chain risk” – the first time this designation has been applied to a US company – in the wake of disputes over how its AI systems can be used by the military. Anthropic has launched a lawsuit over the designation.

China’s military AI arsenal

China’s People’s Liberation Army is racing to incorporate AI , according to three researchers at Georgetown University’s Center for Security and Emerging Technology. “The breadth of its efforts to integrate artificial intelligence into its military and the speed of its experimentation are striking,” they write. Much of China’s work on automated targeting and cyberoperations has parallels in the US, leading to a race that could determine the future of warfare.

AIs go nuclear in war simulations

When large language models from OpenAI, Google and Anthropic were pitted against each other in war game simulations by strategy researcher Kenneth Payne, 95% of the simulations involved at least one nuclear launch . The unsettling result feeds into researchers’ worries that AIs don’t have the same perception of high stakes scenarios as humans and may contribute to conflict escalation.

Vibe coding intelligence dashboards

People are using AI agents to pull together open-source intelligence about the conflict in Iran , including satellite imagery, ship tracking and prediction markets. Observers are concerned these dashboards create an illusion of comprehensive knowledge without accuracy or context.

AI boosts diagnostic accuracy in radiology St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital investigators are deploying AI across radiology to enhance diagnostics, standardize workflows and improve patient care. Studies show that AI assistance significantly improves the detection of pulmonary embolisms and reduces errors in cancer treatment assessments. Machine learning models predict tumour characteristics from MRI data, expanding radiogenomics applications. AI also accelerates image acquisition, reducing scan times for children, and enables 3D tumour modelling for surgical planning and patient education. The goal is human-AI collaboration that enhances clinical judgment rather than replaces it. Tech firm Anthropic is a leader in investigations into how, exactly, generative AI works and what it might be ‘thinking’ — its researchers do everything from examining AI ‘neurons’ to talking to the models the same way a therapist might. Journalist Gideon Lewis-Kraus takes us on a long and insightful tour of the company’s origins , the philosophy of consciousness, and how an experimental AI-powered vending machine in Anthropic’s cafeteria frequently goes off the rails — often, for example, making payments to a Venmo account it hallucinated.

A field guide to AI

Picking the right AI for a task these days involves three decisions : which model (the brains behind the AI, such as GPT-5.3 for example), which app (the interface to the model, such as ChatGPT.com for example) and which harness (the things that enable the AI to do real work, such as OpenClaw). Artificial intelligence researcher and author Ethan Mollik lays out this landscape in a practical guide.

Is AI as smart as a scientist?

Researchers keep developing benchmark tests to evaluate just how intelligent artificial intelligence is, and whether it can put its knowledge to use in the same way as a scientist does. These tests include Humanity’s Last Exam, published in Nature last month, as well as FrontierScience, the Scientific Discovery Evaluation and LABBench2. Results of leading AIs on these benchmarks are mixed, and no one test can fully capture the skills needed to conduct science.
