Vaping probably causes cancer

Nicotine-containing vapes are likely to cause cancer in humans, just like they do in mice , according to a review of case reports, mouse studies and human biomarker studies from 2017 to 2025. This is “by far the strongest evidence” that vapes likely cause oral and lung cancer, says cancer researcher Bernard Stewart who co-authored the study. Around 23% of mice that inhale e-cigarette vapor later develop lung cancer. Many compounds in vapes have been shown to be carcinogenic, including formaldehyde, benzene and nickel. People who vape have elevated levels of toxic chemicals in their urine and have tissue injury, oxidative stress markers and DNA damage that are associated with tumours. There are two case reports of people who have developed oral cancer after chronic e-cigarette use.

49-year-old asbestos article retracted

The Lancet has retracted an influential commentary article published in 1977 that claims asbestos in talcum powder is not a health hazard and does not cause cancer. Two public health historians, David Rosner and Gerald Markowitz, discovered that the article’s anonymous author was actually Francis Roe, a paid consultant working for talcum-powder manufacturer Johnson & Johnson and that Roe had sought feedback from the company prior to publication. “This unsigned commentary has been used to justify the continued marketing of cosmetic talc for nearly half a century,” write the two historians .

Agent Orange finding could help veterans

Researchers have confirmed that people who were exposed to the herbicide Agent Orange during the Vietnam War are at greater risk of developing blood c ancers called myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS). Currently, the US government doesn’t recognize Agent Orange as a cause of MDS, curtailing benefits for veterans with this condition. The finding could help veterans with blood cancer access disability compensation from the government, says haematologist-oncologist and study co-author Mikkael Sekeres. “We’ve long suspected that [Agent Orange] could lead to MDS, but the military and no formal study had ever made the connection before,” says Sekeres.

Non-coding RNA’s role in cancer

The vast majority of the human genome doesn’t code for proteins and was long thought of as ‘junk DNA’. Researchers now know that these non-coding sequences have a range of purposes: forming microRNA that regulate genes, circular RNA that seem to soak up regulatory molecules, piRNA that defend against jumping genes and small interfering RNA that attack specific messenger RNA. These mRNA strands can also promote or suppress cancer . MicroRNA-21, for example, is overexpressed by some cancers, including glioblastoma. A circular RNA called MALAT1 is linked with several types of cancer and is overproduced by tumours.
