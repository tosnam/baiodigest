The therapeutic potential of adhesion GPCRs Adhesion G-protein-coupled receptors (aGPCRs) have immense potential as therapeutics, argue pharmacologist Jin-Peng Sun, medical researcher Peng Xiao and biochemist Ines Liebscher. aGPCRs are membrane proteins with two signalling pathways (cis pictured left, trans right) implicated in many diseases such as cancer, psychiatric disorders and autoimmune disease. No aGPCR-targetting drugs are currently approved, but a number of animal studies have shown promise as well as one human study. To realise the target's potential will involve continued exploration of its diverse signalling pathways, pharmacodynamics and other drug development considerations, write the authors. ( 45 min read | Nature Reviews Drug Discovery ) Features and opinion The key to discovery of RNA-based drugs

A research program in China aims to link the study of RNA biology to drug discovery, to fast-track a shift towards more RNA-based drugs. Despite the success of mRNA vaccines and RNA interference-based drugs, drug development in the area has faced consistent roadblocks: the properties of RNA can contribute to drug toxicity and finding targets poses a challenge . RNA-based drugs also struggle with delivery, particularly outside of the liver. The National Natural Science Foundation of China aims to support cross-disciplinary research aimed at producing RNA-centered drugs. To overcome bottlenecks, researchers need to focus on RNA research aimed at drug production, collaborate across chemistry, materials science and bioengineering and use AI, argue the authors of this comment in Nature Chemical Biology .
