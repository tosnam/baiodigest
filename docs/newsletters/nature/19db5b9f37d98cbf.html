Split opinion on anti-amyloid drugs

A review published by Cochrane, an international group of health researchers, has concluded that the benefits of drugs for Alzheimer’s that target amyloid plaques in the brain are negligible. But some experts have criticised the review for lumping drugs together that work differently and painting them all with the same brush; particularly Leqembi and Kisunla, which were recently approved by the US Food and Drug Administration. “They took some of the rotten ingredients and mixed it in with the fresh food, and the result is a stinky stew,” says neurologist Jason Karlawish.

NMR is coming back into vogue

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) could be on the brink of a renaissance among drug developers. For decades, other techniques that assess the structure of drugs — such as X-ray crystallography and, more recently, cryo-electron microscopy — have overshadowed NMR because of its complexity and slowness. But over the past five years, a suite of contract research organizations (which manage research for drug companies) have been founded with the aim of bringing NMR back into the limelight as the technology improves, to help drug discovery. Meanwhile, NMR has been used to uncover a completely new conformational state in a group of proteins strongly linked with cancer progression . The newly discovered ‘invisible fold’ in kinases could help make kinase inhibitors more selective. “Conformational dynamics in drug discovery are critical and underappreciated,” says venture capitalist Bob Copeland. “This work puts them front and center.”

Off-label cancer drugs don’t often work

The results of an almost decade-long trial have shown that ‘off-label’ cancer therapies — those that are used for tumours other than the ones they’re approved for, often as a last resort — aren't very effective in most cases . There were some positive results, says oncologist and study co-author Emile Voest: around 7% of people in the study responded well to an off-label treatment, some of whom can be considered ‘cured’. But it’s “humbling to see that 93% of the patients did not benefit that much” yet many still experienced side effects, he says. “We have an obligation to really share that data”, because the effects of off-label treatments aren’t usually reported.

CAR-T for autoimmunity is on track for 2027

“We are approaching the first regulatory approval of a CAR-T-cell therapy for autoimmune disease, anticipated within the next year, followed by several more through into 2028,” write translational scientists and company heads Gwendolyn Binder and Raphaël Ognar. The hope is that these will improve on s tandard therapies for autoimmune diseases , which tend to rely on the broad modulation of the immune system and the use of corticosteroids, and rarely cure people. But proof-of-concept studies of CAR-T cells produced outside the body need to translate into solid later-stage clinical data, say the authors. Exploring the new generation of CAR-T cells produced inside the body could help reduce costs and complexity to help scale this therapeutic approach, they add.

How to bring bespoke gene editing to all

In February, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) proposed a radical rethink of how to bring personalized CRISPR therapies, such as the one that saved the life of baby KJ Muldoon last year. Rather than treat each effort as a new drug — which means years of testing at the cost of many millions — drug companies would be able to treat many people with different mutations as part of a single clinical trial. Biomedical researcher Fyodor Urnov and Sadik Kassim, a biotechnology executive and scientist, advise how the FDA can make the most of this plan by ramping its resources, engaging with patient advocates and ensuring that supply chains, insurers and manufacturing capacity are all in place.
