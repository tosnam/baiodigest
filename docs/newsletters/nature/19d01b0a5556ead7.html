Space supercharges anti-bacterial viruses

Viruses that infect bacteria, called phages, evolve different strategies to infect their targets on the International Space Station than they do on the ground, which could help create new treatments for antibiotic-resistant infections. Researchers found that the phages took longer to infect E.coli in microgravity, and that the viruses developed microgravity-specific mutations , some of which helped them to better cling onto bacterial receptors. Once they returned to earth, they were able to kill stubborn strains of E.coli responsible for urinary tract infections that tend to be resistant to bacteriophages.

Tick protein lands one-two immune punch

Researchers have found a protein in tick saliva that blocks chemical signals called chemokines that drive inflammation in the body. When chemokines go haywire, they can cause inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis and cancer. Proteins that block one type of chemokine have already been discovered in tick saliva, but immune conditions often involve two types — called CC and CXC — and this new protein blocks both. The novel shape of the double-action protein allows it to grip onto both types , offering a template for designing broader anti-inflammatory therapies.

Anti-virals put the crosshairs on HIV

A new approach to HIV treatment that triggers infected cells to self-destruct, destroying the virus in the process, has shown promise in lab studies and some early trials. The treatment, which uses two existing anti-viral drugs, forces HIV to assemble an enzyme that the virus usually keeps broken into two halves. This enzyme triggers an internal alarm in cells where HIV persists — T cells and macrophages — which activates a cell-death pathway. The results were presented last month at the Conference on Retroviruses and Other Opportunistic Infections in Denver, Colorado.

Start-up aims to stop cancer spread A McMaster University start-up is developing a new treatment to prevent cancer from spreading to the brain , an innovation that could revolutionize the future of cancer care. The Block Biosciences team is working on a new, first-in-class precision medicine that would be taken proactively by patients after a diagnosis of lung, breast or skin cancer, to prevent secondary metastatic brain cancer, which happens when cancer cells from another part of the body travel to the brain. Features and opinion Jumping genes seize the biopharma pipeline

Most of the world’s biological medicines — such as insulin — are grown inside Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cells: the workhorse of biopharma since the 1980s. But as drugs get more elaborate, genetically engineering CHO cells to reliably produce biomedicines is becoming a bottleneck. Even when using precise gene editing tools, such as CRISPR-Cas9, the DNA repair process needed to successfully insert a new gene only works in around 1 in every 100,000 cells. But transposons — or ‘jumping genes ’ — are directly cut and paste d into genomes by transposase enzymes with relative ease . Newer versions of these enzymes can now stack multiple gene edits into one CHO cell.

All good vaccinology comes in threes

In 2000, researchers debuted ‘reverse vaccinology’, a technique that aims to identify protective antigens in the genomic sequence of a pathogen for use in a vaccine. Then came ‘reverse vaccinology 2.0’, which uses antibodies isolated from people with infections to discover protective antigens and work out the structure of antigen-antibody complexes. Now, immunologists Emanuele Andreano and Rino Rappuoli and structural biologist Jason McLellan have described ‘reverse vaccinology 3.0’, which combines the previous two iterations with AI-enabled structural biology to accelerate the discovery of useful antigens from a years-long search to a matter of days.

Faecal transplants boost immunotherapy

Three trials provide compelling evidence that faecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) can boost the effectiveness of immunotherapy in advanced solid tumors, write oncologists Sumanta Pal and Diwakar Davar in an analysis of three studies published in Nature Medicine . But challenges for safety, donor selection and product development remain. Tumours partially or completely shrunk in 75% of FMT-treated people who underwent immunotherapy with melanoma , 80% of those with non-small cell lung cancer and 50% with metastatic renal cell carcinoma (RCC) across the studies. The only randomized, placebo-controlled trial, which evaluated immunotherapy plus an oral drug with or without FMT in individuals with metastatic RCC, did not find statistically significant 12-month progression-free survival. But it did find that FMT increased the proportion of people whose tumours shrunk (52% versus 32%).
