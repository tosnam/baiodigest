Duikers could be spreading mpox virus

Researchers have found evidence that duikers (Cephalophinae) can be infected with the virus that causes mpox. The team found traces of the virus’s DNA in duiker meat being sold at markets in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, which could suggest that the animals are a source of spillover events . Duikers might also have sparked an outbreak of mpox in chimpanzees in 2017, the team says — there is some evidence that an affected chimpanzee had eaten a duiker or its carcass shortly before it tested positive for the virus.

How sleeping-sickness parasites stay hidden

Trypanosoma brucei — the parasite that causes trypanosomiasis, or sleeping sickness — uses a protein barrier as an ‘invisibility cloak’ to hide from its host’s immune system. This barrier is made of just one type of surface protein out of thousands of possibilities, a uniformity that the parasites achieve by using a special protein as a ‘molecular shredder’ . The shredder destroys messenger RNA molecules that carry instructions to make alternative surface proteins, leaving only those that encode the necessary protein. “The parasite’s secret to staying invisible isn’t just what it prints, but what it chooses to redact,” says biologist and study co-author Joana Faria.

Reprieve from baffling US bird flu outbreak

Two years since the first cases of H5N1 influenza virus were detected in US cows, many questions remain unanswered . Milking machines, milk, bull semen, flies and even the wind have been flagged as possible mechanisms that spread the infection. The outbreak seems to be waning, partly thanks to rules that restrict the movements of lactating cows and a milk-testing programme to pinpoint affected farms. Several promising vaccine candidates are also under development, but it’s not clear if they will be embraced by regulators and farmers.

Symbiont dampens fungal disease

A bacterial symbiont seems to reduce the virulence of Pseudocercospora griseola , the fungus that causes the bean-crop disease angular leaf spot. Researchers found the DNA of the bacterium Achromobacter xylosoxidans in seven isolates of the fungus, all of which caused mild disease symptoms in plants. Other isolates that were highly virulent didn’t house the symbiont . The team also identified a number of ‘jumping genes’ — those that can move between different areas of the genome — in the fungus, which could explain why the species is so adaptable to new environments.

Fungus lovers fight back against invaders

Since the early 2000s, the invasive golden oyster mushroom ( Pleurotus citrinopileatus ) has been wreaking havoc on forest ecosystems in the United States. The fast-growing fungus muscles out native species and destroys the habitats of some animals by breaking down dead wood. The mushroom has also taken hold in some areas of Europe, alongside other invasive species such as the slender golden bolete ( Aureoboletus projectellus ). To give native species a fighting chance, fungi enthusiasts — experts and citizen scientists alike — are cloning them to preserve their genetics and spreading the duplicates to bolster local populations.

The ‘third kingdom’ needs recognition

Researchers estimate that there are anywhere between 2 and 12 million species of fungi on Earth, but have only described around 155,000 of them. This huge knowledge gap leaves mycologists with little to no data on how abundant certain species are and the roles they play in their respective ecosystems. To fill it, advocates are campaigning for fungal species to be recognized as equally important as plants and animals . Their efforts could bear fruit at the COP17 Biodiversity Conference later this year, at which the Fungal Conservation Pledge will be introduced for formal adoption.
