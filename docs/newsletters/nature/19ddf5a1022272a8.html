Bacterial lipid boosts babies’ brain growth

Vaginal bacteria on the skin of newborns produce a lipid that appears to support early brain development. Researchers found that two species, Lactobacillus crispatus and Bacteroides fragilis , work in tandem to produce a fatty molecule called N-bc2S1P. In baby mice, the team found that this lipid triggers some signalling between neurons and recruits proteins involved in gene expression and cell growth in the brain . The findings show that “we should be looking at different niches of the body” that can be colonized with beneficial microbes other than the gut, says microbiome researcher Suchitra Hourigan.

Lung infections stop cancer taking hold

In mice, infections with respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) appear to stop breast cancer from spreading to the lungs. Researchers found that type I interferons — immune proteins that stop a virus from replicating — block cancer cells circulating in the blood from setting up camp in the cells that line the lungs . Giving mice type 1 interferons directly had the same protective effect, which suggests that such treatment could be used to prevent cancer spreading into the lungs if it proves effective in people.

Monkeys eat dirt after junk-food binges

Barbary macaques ( Macaca sylvanus ) in Gibraltar have been spotted eating soil in what might be an attempt to settle their stomachs. Tourists often feed the monkeys high-fat, sugary foods, which could disrupt the animals’ gut microbiomes . The soil could “buffer their digestive system”, says primate researcher and study co-author Sylvain Lemoine, and the bacteria and minerals in it could help to rebalance the monkeys’ microbiomes. But the behaviour might also be causing the monkeys harm. They often eat dirt from the roadside, which could contain harmful pollutants, says Lemoine.

What keeps flagella spinning

After decades of research, scientists have worked out the molecular structure of every part of bacterial flagella — the tail-like structures that many species use to get around. With all the pieces of the puzzle, researchers finally have a clear view of how bacteria use an extremely efficient movement of protons to keep the appendages spinning . “My lifelong quest is now fulfilled,” said biophysicist Mike Manson. “I finally understand how this thing I’ve been studying for 50 years actually works. That’s about as satisfying as can be.”

Perfect storm of genes boosts cancer risk

Different combinations of human leukocyte antigen (HLA) gene variants and strains of the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) can influence a person’s risk of developing the throat cancer nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC). Researchers identified one particular strain of EBV that boosts NPC risk much more than others, but the presence of certain HLA variants can dampen, or further increase, this effect. “Understanding the mechanisms that underpin host susceptibility and protection opens the door to targeted clinical interventions,” write cancer researchers Colm O’hUigin and Mary Carrington.

Is COVID still a major threat?

Six years on from the beginning of the COVID pandemic, cases of the disease have dwindled. But does that mean we don’t need to worry about it? The answer isn’t clear cut, say experts . Virtually everyone has at least some immunity to SARS-CoV-2 at this point — even those who were never officially infected — and variants that are still circulating only cause mild illness in most people. But not everyone can let their guard down, they say — older adults, children and people with weakened immune systems should still get booster vaccinations as recommended.
