Off-label cancer drugs are hit and miss

Seven percent of people with advanced cancer who are given off-label treatments are alive without cancer progression at least two years on — but around 93% of people are not. “I’m a glass-half-full type of person,” says oncologist Emile Voest who co-authored the study. “So, that is very positive.” It’s also “humbling” to see that so few people gained a substantial benefit from off-label drug usage , he says. In the study of more than 1,600 people with advanced cancer who were given off-label treatment, around one-third saw their tumours partially shrink or remain the same size after four months. Off-label prescribing should only occur inside clinical trials because the side effects can be considerable and only a fraction of people benefit, the authors concluded.

Drug doubles survival for pancreatic cancer

People with advanced pancreatic cancer are twice as likely to be alive after one year if they take a drug called elraglusib. In a study of 286 people, those who took elraglusib in combination with chemotherapy had one-year survival rates of 44% compared with 23% in those who were given chemotherapy alone. Elraglusib inhibits an enzyme called glycogen synthase kinase-3 beta, blocking cancer-cell proliferation and helping immune cells to infiltrate the tumour. “We're starting to see, for the first time, drugs that are effective outside of the standard chemotherapy that we have used for pancreatic cancer for the last two decades,” says study co-author and oncologist Devalingam Mahalingam.

Light-sensitive cancer drugs win $2 million

Researchers have won a US$2-million prize for demonstrating that future quantum computers could simulate the chemical reactions that drive light-sensitive cancer drugs in ways that would be impossible using classical computing. These drugs can be activated using light once they reach a tumour, minimizing toxicity to the rest of the body. The US$2-million Quantum for Bio prize was awarded to researchers from quantum software firm Algorithmiq, technology company IBM and the Cleveland Clinic hospital.

Drugs for platinum-resistant ovarian cancer

An aggressive type of ovarian cancer that is resistant to platinum-based treatment can be treated with a drug called relacorilant or the immunotherapy pembrolizumab . In a study of 381 people with platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, those taking relacorilant alongside chemotherapy lived four months longer on average than did those receiving standard treatment. A separate study showed that people with platinum-resistant ovarian cancer lived an average of 17.7 months when given pembrolizumab alongside standard care, and 14 months when given standard care alone. Both treatments have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration for use in people with platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, but further testing is required before the drugs can be approved for this use in other countries.

Genetics redefine childhood thyroid cancer St. Jude researchers have uncovered new genetic clues behind paediatric thyroid cancer. By closely examining a well-characterized group of young patients, the team found that roughly one-quarter carried mutations in known cancer predisposition genes, with younger patients more likely to have inherited risk. The findings suggest that thyroid cancer in children is more genetically driven than in adults , strengthening the case for routine genetic testing to help doctors to plan treatment and monitor children more effectively. Some countries, including Australia, Canada, South Korea and the United States, have responded to the rise in cancers in younger people by lowering the age of screening . However, cancer screening carries a risk of false positives and injury through unnecessary procedures, so “there really does need to be a certain prevalence of cancer in the population to justify inviting asymptomatic individuals to undergo screening,” says cancer epidemiologist Robert Smith. Cancers in people aged under 50 are definitely increasing compared with five years ago. “I see it every day. I have way too many patients who are my age — I’m 37 — or even younger, and it’s horrible,” says medical oncologist Yoanna Pumpalova. But it is possible that more-sensitive screening could be driving this trend.

Discarded ‘salt’ leaves trail of cancers

In the 1980s, a US oil company called Amoco, now owned by BP, searched for oil in the Kenyan village of Kargi by drilling nearly a dozen wells. After five years, the company abandoned the effort and left behind a white substance near the wells that people relied on for water. The local community assumed this industrial byproduct was salt and used it to prepare food. But it was contaminated with heavy metals and carcinogens that might have contributed to the deaths of livestock and higher r ates of stomach and throat cancer in people living there, according to an investigation by The Intercept .
