Cellular scaffold helps some tumours thrive

When tumours first take hold, they send distress signals to nearby cells called fibroblasts, which triggers the cells to form protective scaffolds around them. Researchers found that when they blocked the distress signals, these support structures didn’t form and fewer tumours survived . The team also found that fibroblast scaffolds can give healthy, non-mutated cells tumour-like properties. “Although the clinical aspects of our work are at a very early stage, it has given us some biomarkers — red flags — that could help identify cancer much earlier,” says biomedical scientist Greta Skrupskelyte.

Breast cancer pill falls at the final hurdle

The oral selective estrogen receptor degrader (SERD) drug giredestrant — which was poised to be drug company Roche’s biggest seller — has fallen short in a phase III clinical trial as a first-line treatment. The drug was tested in combination with Pfizer’s targeted therapy Ibrance in patients with locally advanced or metastatic estrogen receptor-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer. It showed no statistically significant improvement in progression-free survival over the control group, which received Ibrance and hormone therapy. Sales of the drug were forecast to reach US$7.4 billion by 2034. The failure “pulls the rug from [Roche’s] multi-billion dollar giredestrant case”, according to analysts from the investment banking firm Jefferies.

Ivermectin: cancer’s new snake oil

Anti-parasitic drug ivermectin is gaining traction in the United States as a treatment for cancer, despite a lack of evidence that it can do so. Five states — Arkansas, Idaho, Louisiana, Tennessee and Texas — have voted to give people over-the-counter access to the drug, fueling concerns among doctors that it could be misused. The furor echoes a similar misinformation-fueled uptick in ivermectin use during the COVID-19 pandemic.

All anticoagulants bleed the same

In a retrospective study of almost 3,000 adults with advanced prostate cancer taking anti-cancer drugs between 2012 and 2023, there was no increased risk of clotting or bleeding in those taking direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) — a newer class of blood thinner that targets specific clotting factors — compared with those taking other anticoagulants, despite previous results that raised concerns. “Our findings suggest that pharmacokinetic drug–drug interaction concerns may not translate into adverse clinical outcomes in the real world,” says haematologist and first author Tzu-Fei Wang.

Lasers help immunotherapy reach the brain

Researchers have found a way to disrupt the blood-brain barrier using lasers, which helps immunotherapy drugs reach the brain. Typically, immune checkpoint inhibitors have failed against brain cancers like astrocytoma, which are aggressive and often reoccur, because the layer of cells between the bloodstream and the brain stops immune cells reaching the tumour. In a phase I/IIb clinical trial of 45 people with recurrent astrocytoma, nearly half of all participants treated with laser interstitial thermal therapy (LITT) followed by the immune checkpoint inhibitor pembrolizumab were still alive at 18 months. More than one-third of patients treated with LITT and the immune checkpoint inhibitor lived for more than three years , much longer than the expected prognosis of four-to-five months.

Include Latin America in clinical oncology

Inclusive clinical trial designs are essential to reduce disparities and bolster the role of Latin America in global oncology research, argues clinical researcher Andreia Cristina de Melo. Currently, less than 5% of global cancer trials actively recruit patients in Latin America . Physicians, advocacy groups and sponsors must collaborate to build public trust in clinical trials, provide infrastructure and expand eligibility criteria to make sure that typically underrepresented Latin-American populations are included. With the burden of cancer-related deaths in the region expected to double by 2050, it is time that the area be recognised for its potential to further clinical research, writes de Melo.

Cancer blood tests: caveat emptor

You might have heard of blood tests that promise to detect the early signs of many types of cancer, or to provide the reassurance of an all-clear — one was even advertised during American football’s Super Bowl last month. But few of the 40 such tests that are either in development or already on sale have been through randomized controlled trials, and none has yet received approval from regulators . Critics point out that the tests fail to detect many early cancers, and that the downsides of false diagnoses for many people might outweigh the benefits for a few. “I don’t think we’re anywhere close to ready for widespread adoption, but it’s an exciting technology,” says public-health physician Tom Callender. “If we do it properly, [it] could be an important part of the mix in the future.”
