KRAS drugs enter clinical trials

Several clinical trials are underway to test next-generation drugs that stop mutated KRAS proteins from driving cancer . These drugs fit into two groups: the ‘degraders’, which trick the cell into treating the mutant proteins like rubbish, and the ‘inhibitors’, which block KRAS activity. This year, the first KRAS degrader, called setidegrasib, had some success in a clinical trial. Four large clinical trials are also testing KRAS inhibitors, including the drug daraxonrasib, manufactured by Revolution Medicines.

Drug might shrink ovarian cancer

A drug called Mocertatug Rezetecan, or Mo-Rez, might shrink or eliminate tumours in more than 60% of individuals with endometrial cancer or chemotherapy-resistant ovarian cancer , its manufacturer GSK reports. Over the past year, the antibody–drug conjugate was trialled in 224 people with endometrial cancer or ovarian cancer where chemotherapy had failed, and showed promising results and limited side effects. GSK reported the unpublished results at a Society of Gynecologic Oncology’s annual meeting on women’s cancer in Puerto Rico. In the coming months, the drug will be tested in five, late-stage clinical trials.

Married couples have lower cancer risks

People who have never been married are more likely to get cancer than people who have been (or are) married . In a study of more than 4 million cancer cases, the risk of cancer was 68% higher in never-married men and 85% higher in never-married women. Tying the knot does not affect cancer biology in itself, but other factors, such as smoking, stress and giving birth could be linked with marriage and might drive the association with cancer, report the researchers. “It means that if you're not married, you should be paying extra attention to cancer risk factors, getting any screenings you may need, and staying up to date on health care,” says clinical psychologist Frank Penedo.

Blood test could spot leukaemia sooner Spotting early signs of leukaemia in children can save lives — but it usually means early, invasive bone marrow exams. St. Jude investigators have shown there’s a gentler way . By analysing a simple blood sample, they detected pre-leukaemic clones with high accuracy, picking up genetic changes present in as little as 1% of cells. The approach helped clinicians intervene earlier in at-risk patients, and could complement or reduce reliance on bone marrow sampling. While not perfect for every chromosomal change, the study supports a more frequent, less invasive surveillance strategy for high-risk children. Ben Riley, a self-taught cognitive scientist, created a newsletter to ‘explain AI to the average Joe’. His father, Joe, was one of his first subscribers. Ben was therefore shocked — and could see the irony — when he discovered that his dad, a retired neuroscientist, had been using the artificial intelligence chatbot Perplexity to justify his decision to refuse treatment for a type of leukaemia . The information generated by Perplexity was misleading, according to three doctors that Ben consulted. “Do you really think you know more than all of them because of this stupid AI report?” he asked his father. “Yes,” Joe replied. Joe died from cancer late last year. Ben believes psychology played a role in his father’s death but AI wasn’t blameless; it produced misinformation wrapped in scientific packaging.

Mastectomy pain swept under the rug

Sophia Bassan thought she was losing her mind when she experienced debilitating pain for months after undergoing a mastectomy. “One time I was in so much pain that I had to take off my top, and then my cat’s tail brushed against my back. I screamed,” she says. Bassan had to sleep sitting up because lying down was too painful. Post-mastectomy pain syndrome affects an estimated 10-50% of people who undergo the procedure and can last for years. But women are often not warned about this risk by their surgeons and feel dismissed when they have complications. “Doctors act like it is so rare, like they’re so baffled,” says Bassan. “But this is statistically predictable.”
