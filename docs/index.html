Nature Briefing: Microbiology

Researchers have found evidence that duikers (Cephalophinae) can be infected with the virus that causes mpox. The team found traces of the virus’s DNA in duiker meat being sold at markets in the Democratic Republic of the Congo, which could suggest that the animals are a source of spillover events . Duikers might also have sparked an outbreak of mpox in chimpanzees in 2017, the team says — there is some evidence that an affected chimpanzee had eaten a duiker or its carcass shortly before it tested positive for the virus.

Nature Briefing: Microbiology

Tiny ‘spikes’ on the scales of ball pythons ( Python regius ) could protect the snakes from bacterial infections. Researchers found that these spikes, each around the size of an individual cell, prevent the formation of biofilms — colonies of microbes that live co-operatively in a slimy matrix. The team theorizes that the protrusions might make it harder for bacteria to stick to the scales, or could force them into spatial arrangements that aren’t conducive to forming biofilms.

Nature Briefing

Researchers have resurrected ‘dead’ bacterial cells by replacing their defunct DNA with the working genome of another species. The functionally dead Mycoplasma capricolum received engineered genomes from the closely-related Mycoplasma mycoides . If researchers can routinely make zombies from more diverse bacteria, the technique could open the door to re-engineered microbial life imbued with useful properties , such as the ability to make drugs or biofuels. For instance, it could help transform a laboratory staple, such as Escherichia coli , into “a general-purpose platform for synthetic biology”, suggests synthetic biologist Olivier Borkowski.

Nature Briefing: AI & Robotics

Australian data analyst Paul Conyngham turned to a chatbot to help design a personalized vaccine for his dog’s cancer . His five-year-old pet Rosie was given only months to live, facing a tennis-ball-sized mast cell tumour on her leg. Conyngham paid a university genomicist to sequence Rosie’s tumour and used ChatGPT to identify relevant tumour-specific proteins. He then commissioned a university biochemist to design a personalized mRNA vaccine to promote an immune response. Rosie’s condition improved, though it’s unclear whether it was the vaccine or a more general cancer treatment that helped most. Similar treatments are in clinical trials for people with lung cancer.

Nature Briefing: Microbiology

A newly described species of ‘magic mushroom’ could upend a popular theory of when psychedelic fungi popped up around the world. Researchers had thought that the magic mushrooms that grow in southern Africa were Psilocybe cubensis , the same species that grows in the Americas. Closer inspection revealed that the African mushrooms are a separate species, now named Psilocybe ochraceocentrata , and last shared a common ancestor with P. cubensis approximately 1.5 million years ago. These findings scupper the hypothesis that P. cubensis was inadvertently introduced to the Americas by 16th-century settlers , but the research offers no clues as to its origins across the Atlantic.

Nature Briefing: Translational Research

Viruses that infect bacteria, called phages, evolve different strategies to infect their targets on the International Space Station than they do on the ground, which could help create new treatments for antibiotic-resistant infections. Researchers found that the phages took longer to infect E.coli in microgravity, and that the viruses developed microgravity-specific mutations , some of which helped them to better cling onto bacterial receptors. Once they returned to earth, they were able to kill stubborn strains of E.coli responsible for urinary tract infections that tend to be resistant to bacteriophages.

Nature Briefing: Microbiology

If a person has a peanut allergy, the make up of their gut and oral microbiomes might influence the severity of their reaction. Researchers found that certain species of Rothia bacteria in the mouth and small intestine can break down two proteins in peanuts that trigger an allergic reaction . These bacteria were more abundant in the microbiomes of people with mild peanut allergies than in those with severe ones. The team suggests that Rothia probiotics could one day be used to reduce the severity of allergic reactions to peanuts.

When tumours first take hold, they send distress signals to nearby cells called fibroblasts, which triggers the cells to form protective scaffolds around them. Researchers found that when they blocked the distress signals, these support structures didn’t form and fewer tumours survived . The team also found that fibroblast scaffolds can give healthy, non-mutated cells tumour-like properties. “Although the clinical aspects of our work are at a very early stage, it has given us some biomarkers — red flags — that could help identify cancer much earlier,” says biomedical scientist Greta Skrupskelyte.

Nature Briefing: Translational Research

A type of immunotherapy called a T-cell engager, which uses an engineered antibody to connect T cells to tumour cells, has shrunk hard-to-treat prostate tumours in an early-stage trial. Researchers tested the therapy, called VIR-5500, on 58 people with advanced, treatment-resistant prostate cancer and found that tumours shrank in five out of 11 people who were given the highest dose. One 63-year-old study participant had 14 liver metastases disappear after six rounds of treatment. The study has not yet been peer reviewed and was presented at the American Society of Clinical Oncology genitourinary cancers symposium. “We do need more data but the results are stunning,” says cancer researcher and co-author Johann de Bono.

Nature Briefing

The rate of global warming has surged since 2015 and is now nearly double what it was in the 1970s, according to a new study. That’s faster than some other estimates, but the authors say their analysis captures a more accurate picture because it accounts for the effects of natural factors such as the El Niño weather pattern. Most climate scientists agree that warming is accelerating — partly because of a reduction in sun-reflecting air pollution — but some have questioned this study’s results. The authors’ methods for removing natural fluctuations are “imperfect and will leave some remaining effects”, says climate scientist Zeke Hausfather.
